# Japanese Keigo (敬語) Comprehensive Research Guide

## Executive Summary

This document provides an exhaustive research into Japanese keigo (敬語), the honorific language system that governs politeness, social hierarchy, and formal speech in Japan. The research covers all major categories, real example sentences, common errors, regional variations, and modern trends.

## PART 1: THE FOUR MAJOR LEVELS OF KEIGO

### 1. TEINEIGO (丁寧語) — Polite Language

**Definition:** The foundational polite register using desu (です) and masu (ます) endings. Applies general politeness without specifically elevating or lowering anyone.

**When to use:**
- First-time interactions with strangers
- Commercial settings (stores, restaurants)
- Workplace formal situations
- When you don't know the person's status

**Key markers:**
- Verb endings: ます (masu), ました (mashita), ません (masen)
- Noun/adjective endings: です (desu), でした (deshita)
- ございます (gozaimasu) — more formal version of desu

#### Example Sentences:

| Japanese | Romanization | English |
|----------|--------------|---------|
|  저는 학생입니다。 | Watashi wa gakusei desu. | I am a student. |
|  일본어를勉強합니다。 | Nihongo wo benkyou shimasu. | I study Japanese. |
|  이것은 펜입니다。 | Kore wa pen desu. | This is a pen. |
|  감사합니다。 | Arigatou gozaimasu. | Thank you very much. |
|  실례합니다。 | Shitsurei shimasu. | Excuse me. |
|  네, 알겠습니다。 | Hai, wakarimashita. | Yes, I understand. |

### 2. SONKEIGO (尊敬語) — Respectful Language

**Definition:** Honorific language that **elevates the actions of the listener or someone being discussed** (not the speaker). Shows respect to customers, bosses, teachers, or anyone of higher social status.

**When to use:**
- Talking about a superior's actions
- Addressing customers in service industry
- Business meetings with clients
- Referring to someone's accomplishments

**Three formation methods:**

#### Method 1: Passive Form
Many Japanese verbs in passive form also function as sonkeigo.

| Plain | Passive/Sonkeigo | English |
|-------|------------------|---------|
| 食べる | 食べられる | to eat (respectful) |
| 行く | 行かれる | to go (respectful) |
| 見る | 見られる | to see (respectful) |

#### Method 2: お + Verb Stem + になる

| Plain | Sonkeigo | English |
|-------|---------|---------|
| 書く | お書きになる | to write |
| 読む | お読みになる | to read |
| 待つ | お待ちになる | to wait |
| 話す | お話になる | to speak |
| 利用する | ご利用になる | to use (kango) |

#### Method 3: Special Sonkeigo Verbs (Must Memorize)

These irregular verbs are essential:

| Plain | Sonkeigo | Romanization | English |
|-------|----------|--------------|---------|
| 行く・来る・いる | いらっしゃる | irassharu | to go/come/exist |
| する | なさる | nasaru | to do |
| 言う | おっしゃる | ossharu | to say |
| 食べる・飲む | 召し上がる | meshiagaru | to eat/drink |
| 見る | ご覧になる | goran ni naru | to see |
| 知っている | ございます | gozonza suru | to know |
| 座位る | 掛ける | kakeru | to sit |

### 3. KENJOUGO (謙譲語) — Humble Language

**Definition:** Language that **lowers the speaker's own actions** to create contrast with the elevated listener. Used when talking about yourself or your in-group.

**When to use:**
- Talking about your own actions to superiors
- Business emails and formal correspondence
- Self-introductions in professional settings
- When visiting someone's place of business

**Key concept: Uchi (内) vs. Soto (外)**
- **Uchi (内)** — Your in-group: family, company, team
- **Soto (外)** — Out-group: clients, customers, external parties

**Critical rule:** When speaking to someone in the soto, humble everyone in your uchi — regardless of their actual rank.

#### Formation Methods:

##### Method 1: お + Verb Stem + する/致す

| Plain | Kenjougo | English |
|-------|----------|---------|
| 書く | お書きする | to write |
| 送る | お送りする | to send |
| 待つ | お待ちする | to wait |
| 説明する | ご説明説明する | to explain |

##### Method 2: Special Humble Verbs

| Plain | Kenjougo | Romanization | English |
|-------|----------|--------------|---------|
| 行く来る | 参る | mairu | to go/come |
| する | 致す | itasu | to do |
| 言う | 申す | mousu | to say |
| 食べる飲む | いただく | itadaku | to eat/drink |
| 見る | 拝見する | haiken suru | to see |
| もらう | 雰囲いただく | uketamawaru | to receive |

| 知らせる | ご存知です | zonjiru | to know |

### 4. BIKAGO (美白語) — Beautified Language

**Definition:** Language that "beautifies" inanimate objects. Uses お (o) or ご (go) prefixes to make nouns sound more elegant or refined. Unlike the other keigo forms, bikago can be used when talking about oneself.

**When to use:**
- In restaurants (ordering food)
- Talking about everyday objects
- To make speech sound more refined
- In polite service contexts

**Prefix rules:**
- お (o) — Native Japanese words (wago)
- ご (go) — Chinese-derived words (kango)

| Noun | Bikago | English |
|------|-------|---------|
| 茶 | お茶 | tea |
| 金 | お金 | money |
| 水 | お水 | water |
| 飯 | お飯 | rice |
| 電話 | お電話 | telephone |
| サーリー | 、ご案內 | guidance |
| 注文 | ご注文 | order |

#### Key Difference from Sonkeigo/Kenjougo:

Bikago can be used even when referring to your own possessions or actions:

| Japanese | Romanization | English |
|----------|--------------|---------|
|  제가 사용할게요.| Watashi no okae wo tsukaimasu. | I will use my money. |
|  물을 마실래요. | Ommizu wo nomitai desu. | I want to drink water. |

## PART 3: COMMON MISTAKES FOREIGNERS MAKE

### Mistake #1: Using Sonkeigo for Yourself

**Incorrect:** 社長が参ります。 (Watashi ga shachou ga mairimasu)
**Correct:** 私が参ります。 (Watashi ga mairimasu)

**Problem:** Applying honorific language to yourself is unnatural and sounds bizarre.

### Mistake #2: Mixing Uchi-Soto

**Incorrect:** (To a client about your boss) 社長がいらっしゃいました。
**Correct:** (To a client about your boss) 社長が参りました。

**Problem:** In front of clients (soto), you must humble even your own boss. Using sonkeigo for in-group members when speaking to outsiders is wrong.

### Mistake #3: Using Wrong Verb Forms

**Incorrect:** ハンバーガー定食になります。
**Correct:** ハンバーガー定食でございます。

**Problem:** Using "become" (になる) rather than "to be" (でございます) creates logical错误.

### Mistake #4: Adding Too Many Honorifics

**Incorrect:** お客様のますますおっしゃってください。
**Correct:** お客様，您不舒服.

**Problem:** Stacking honorific prefixes creates ungrammatical sentences.

### Mistake #5: Over-Using Keigo with Friends

**Problem:** Using keigo with close friends creates unnecessary social distance.

### Mistake #6: Forgetting Consistency

**Problem:** Randomly switching between casual and formal mid-conversation sounds careless.

## PART 4: WHEN TO USE EACH LEVEL

### Quick Reference Guide

| Situation | Appropriate Level |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Talking to close friends | Plain form (ダ会話) |
| Coworkers of similar rank | Teineigo |
| First-time business meetings | Kenjougo + Sonkeigo |
| Customer service | Teineigo + Sonkeigo |
| Talking about your boss to clients | Kenjougo |
| Talking about clients to your boss | Sonkeigo |
| Job interviews | Kenjougo + Teineigo |
| Writing business emails | Kenjougo |

### The Gradient of Requests

From casual to maximally formal:

| Level | Japanese | Translation |
|-------|----------|-------------|
| Plain | 確認して | Check this |
| Teineigo | 確認してください | Please check this |
| Business | 確認していただけますか？ | Could you check this? |
| Formal | 確認捅し上げていただければ幸いです | I would be grateful if you could check |
| Maximum | ご確認の上、よろしくお願い申し上げます | I humbly request your verification |

### Cushion Words (クッション言葉)

These soften requests or bad news:

**For requests:**
- 恐れ入りますが… (I'm sorry to impose…)
- お手数をかけますが… (I know it's trouble…)
- よろしければ… (If convenient��)

**For refusals/bad news:**
- あいにくですが… (Unfortunately…)
- 申し訳ありませんが… (I apologize but…)
- 誠に恐れ入りますが… (I'm sincerely sorry but…)

## PART 5: REGIONAL DIFFERENCES — TOKYO VS. OSAKA

### Historical Context

Historically, extensive use of keigo was a feature of the Kansai dialect (especially Kyoto), while the Kantō dialect (Tokyo area) lacked elaborate honorifics. Standard Japanese (共通語/hyōjun-go) was developed based on the Tokyo dialect but incorporated the Kyoto-style keigo system.

### Key Differences

| Feature | Tokyo (標準語) | Osaka (関西弁) |
|---------|--------------|---------------|
| Politeness levels | Complex uchi-soto distinction | Similar but less rigid |
| Copula | です/だ | や/やん |
| Come (kuru) | 来ます | 来はる (kiharu) |
| Plain form after noun | だ (da) | や (ya) |
| Sentence endings | ます/です | やん/ーで |
| Teineigo | です/ます | でっか/～ん |

### Modern Reality

1. **Standard Japanese dominates:** Young people in Osaka primarily use standard keigo in professional settings.
2. **Kansai dialect in casual settings:** Osaka-ben remains popular for informal communication.
3. **Keigo usage is similar:** The actual keigo forms don't differ significantly between regions.
4. **Politeness marking differs:** Kansai uses different sentence-final forms rather than different keigo vocabulary.

### Example Comparison

| Meaning | Tokyo | Osaka |
|---------|-------|-------|
| "It's a pen" | ペンですよ | ペンやん |
| "I'll go" | 行きます | 行かはる |
| "It is" | です | や |

## PART 6: MODERN KEIGO TRENDS AMONG YOUNG PEOPLE

### Statistics (Japan Times, December 2024)

According to a Japanese government survey:
- **82.9%** of Japanese think keigo is important
- **59.5%** feel they cannot use appropriate keigo in formal settings
- **67.5%** of people in their 30s admit difficulty with keigo — the highest uncertainty rate

### Trend #1: Decline of Traditional Keigo

- Younger Japanese increasingly use casual speech in informal settings
- The uchi-soto distinction is becoming blurred
- Teineigo is often the highest level used in daily life

### Trend #2: Rise of "Manual Keigo" (バイト敬語)

This is a simplified, often incorrect keigo taught in employee training manuals at convenience stores and fast-food restaurants.

**Common examples of incorrect "baito keigo":**

| Non-Standard | Problem |
|--------------|---------|
| お釣りは○○円になります | "Become" incorrectly used for "to be" |
| ○○円でございます的正确形式は○○円でございます | Redundant honorifics |
| ご注文は以上でよろしかったですか？ | Wrong tense usage |
| お車のほうは大丈夫でしたか？ | Unnecessary "no-hō" indirection |

### Trend #3: Simplification in Digital Communication

- LINE and social media have casual language
- Email still maintains formal keigo
- Young people can switch between registers contextually

### Trend #4: Regional Dialect Revival

Some young people use Kansai-ben as a stylistic choice, creating ironic distance from standard Japanese.

### Trend #5: Women's Keigo Patterns

Women's speech in Japan traditionally uses softer forms, but this distinction is diminishing among young people, with more neutral speech patterns emerging.

## CONCLUSION: KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **Master teineigo first** — Start with です/ます forms (JLPT N5 level)
2. **Learn special verb pairs** — 参る, 致す, 申す, いただく, 雰囲, おっしゃる
3. **Understand uchi-soto** — The in-group/out-group distinction is fundamental
4. **Don't confuse sonkeigo and kenjougo** — Elevate others, lower yourself
5. **Context matters more than rules** — Observe native speakers in real situations
6. **Japanese people appreciate effort** — Even imperfect keigo is valued
7. **Practice with real scenarios** — Business emails, restaurant orders, phone calls
